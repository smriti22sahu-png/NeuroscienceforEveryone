What is a Neuron?

The basic cell that carries information through the nervous system.

A neuron, or nerve cell, is the basic functional unit of the nervous system. Every thought, movement, sensation, and memory depends on billions of neurons communicating with one another.

Like most cells, neurons contain a nucleus, mitochondria, and other structures needed for survival. However, neurons also possess specialized structures called dendrites and axons that allow them to receive and transmit information.

Neurons are electrically excitable, meaning they can generate and carry signals. They communicate with other cells through structures called synapses. Synapses can be electrical or chemical and allow information to pass from one cell to another.

Why are neurons important?

Neurons make it possible for the brain and nervous system to process information quickly. Without them, learning, memory, and movement would not work in the way we experience them.
